# Architectural Design Specification: Axodus Federated Human-AI Governance Infrastructure

> **Institutional notice:** This is a conceptual architectural specification
> and research agenda. It does not represent an implemented or production-ready
> system, does not constitute scientific validation, and makes no claims about
> tokenomics, financial performance, or operational readiness.

## 1. Document Overview and Strategic Intent

This specification outlines a conceptual framework for examining how the
operational velocity of automated systems might be reconciled with the
requirements of institutional accountability. From a socio-technical
perspective, complex-system failures can involve interactions and control
mismatches between human intent and machine execution rather than isolated
component defects alone [Leveson, 2012](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262533690/engineering-a-safer-world/).

The architecture addresses this design problem by making accountability
assumptions inspectable. It proposes a federated, layered approach in which
local autonomy is bounded by shared constraints and digital activity is
represented as reviewable institutional evidence. These are design objectives,
not claims of achieved accountability, reliability, or institutional use.

### Document scope

This specification is a conceptual reference design and foundational research
agenda based on the Draft v0.6 baseline. It provides structural logic for
governance layers and is not a representation of a validated, production-ready
system.

### Evidence boundaries

The following domains are excluded from this specification:

- **Tokenomics:** no claims regarding token supply, `$Neurons`, or value-accrual
  mechanisms.
- **Financial performance:** no projections of treasury growth, APY, or
  investment returns.
- **Operational deployment:** no claim that the system is operational,
  validated in a live environment, or ready for production use.

### Why this architecture?

The design problem is how to coordinate constitutional constraints, bounded
local authority, modular capabilities, evidence, advisory AI assistance, and
review paths without treating documentation as proof of implementation. The
specification therefore uses six conceptual layers. The layers are one
proposed decomposition among possible alternatives; they are not asserted to
be necessary, minimal, uniquely correct, or universally sufficient.

### Design principles

1. Technical authorization and institutional justification should be recorded
   together as separate conceptual requirements.
2. Local governance should remain bounded by higher-order constraints.
3. Functional capabilities should expose purpose, evidence needs, dependencies,
   escalation, and failure conditions.
4. Evidence and provenance should support reconstruction without being treated
   as proof of legitimacy, integrity, security, or accountability.
5. AI assistance should remain attributable and advisory, with human
   disposition retained.
6. Risk boundaries and contestability should be specified before a decision is
   treated as final.

## 2. Layer 1: Constitutional Governance (Design Rules)

The Constitutional Governance layer is a proposed non-delegable center of the
conceptual model. It defines higher-order design rules that constrain the
infrastructure, including Universal Prohibitions: actions that no agent,
human or AI, is permitted to take within the proposal.

This layer is a design surface, not a deployed constitution, legal instrument,
active authority, enforcement body, or guarantee of correct outcomes.

### Governance parameters

The layer proposes formalizing roles, decision rights, amendment procedures,
review paths, and challenge conditions. Technical authorization, such as a
future cryptographic or procedural gate, must not be treated as a substitute
for institutional justification under the proposed model.

### Core invariant

The following is a proposed architectural invariant:

> Every action treated as valid within the model requires both Technical
> Authorization and Institutional Justification aligned with constitutional
> rules and the evidence and provenance layer.

The invariant is a design requirement for future evaluation, not evidence that
any implementation currently enforces it.

### Maturity taxonomy boundary

L-Level and D-Level are proposed conceptual axes. L-Level concerns
organizational, documentation, governance, and authority maturity. D-Level
concerns development, implementation, technical validation, and operational
maturity. Neither is an empirically validated measurement instrument, and
neither grants production status or sensitive authority.

The controlled repository taxonomy is the source for any future level
definitions: [Axodus internal maturity taxonomy](../../../governance/maturity-taxonomy.md).

## 3. Layer 2: Federated Tenant Governance Domains

Axodus uses the Tenant concept to describe a bounded local governance domain.
The relationship is informed by the idea of polycentric governance, in which
multiple decision centers may operate under shared constraints
[Ostrom, 2010](https://doi.org/10.1257/aer.100.3.641). This is a conceptual
analogy and does not imply a deployed federation, legal federal status,
operating community, or SaaS tenancy.

### Tenant autonomy

Within a bounded local governance domain, a Tenant may configure local
processes and select functional service modules. The proposal limits that
discretion by constitutional constraints and Universal Prohibitions defined in
Layer 1.

The proposed separation is intended to support fault isolation between local
configuration and other governance domains. Whether modular separation
improves resilience or prevents propagation of failures remains a hypothesis
for future testing.

### Interface requirements

The relationship between the Constitutional Center and a Tenant is described
through a proposed Contract of Interface. This contract supplies the semantic
mapping needed to translate local actions into constitutional constraints for
system-wide review.

These configuration choices determine which conceptual functional service
modules are in scope in the following layer.

## 4. Layer 3: Functional Service Modules

Consistent with modularity as a design concern, this layer proposes Functional
Service Modules as discrete conceptual units with standardized interfaces
[Baldwin and Clark, 2000](https://mitpress.mit.edu/9780262538206/design-rules-volume-1/).
The modules are abstractions, not a definitive product inventory, software
components, or implementation claims.

Every module should expose a conceptual field schema: Purpose, Inputs, Outputs,
Dependencies, Escalation, and Failure Behavior.

### Taxonomy of functional classes

1. **Authority and Role Registry**
   - Purpose: manage proposed decision rights, delegations, and role-based
     access.
   - Inputs: constitutional rule-set and Tenant-level role assignments.
   - Outputs: a proposed authorization record for a specific action.
   - Failure behavior: propose deny-all handling and manual constitutional
     review.
2. **Evidence Registry**
   - Purpose: store and reference source materials and reference data.
   - Inputs: raw data entities and cryptographic hashes where a future
     implementation requires them.
   - Outputs: conceptual Entity packages for the provenance layer.
   - Failure behavior: lock the affected conceptual record and flag an
     integrity mismatch for review.
3. **Decision-Rationale Records**
   - Purpose: capture human and AI reasoning associated with an institutional
     action.
   - Inputs: human-provided text and AI-generated summaries.
   - Outputs: a structured Rationale Package linked to a decision ID.
   - Failure behavior: mark the record incomplete and prevent a proposed final
     state transition until a valid rationale is supplied.
4. **Conflict and Disclosure Register**
   - Purpose: track potential biases or interests of human participants.
   - Inputs: role disclosures and third-party interest data.
   - Outputs: a conceptual conflict-status flag such as Clear, Disclosed, or
     Conflict-Found.
   - Failure behavior: default to disclosed conflict found and escalate to an
     independent role if the registry is unreachable.
5. **Review and Appeal Paths**
   - Purpose: define escalation routes for contested decisions.
   - Inputs: risk-boundary triggers or human-initiated challenges.
   - Outputs: assignment of a case to a proposed independent reviewer.
   - Failure behavior: trigger a Governance Exception alert to the proposed
     Constitutional Center.

These behaviors are candidate design rules. They are not implemented controls,
guaranteed defaults, or evidence of security or reliability.

## 5. Layer 4: Evidence and Provenance

Institutional accountability depends in part on reconstructability. The model
uses the W3C PROV-DM vocabulary as a conceptual foundation for describing
entities, activities, agents, derivations, attribution, and responsibility
relationships [W3C, 2013](https://www.w3.org/TR/2013/REC-prov-dm-20130430/).

### Entity-Activity-Agent mapping

Decisions are represented as a conceptual graph in which source documents
(Entities) are transformed through analysis steps (Activities) by human or
automated participants (Agents).

### Traceability schema

The proposed decision record contains seven conceptual fields:

| Field | Conceptual requirement |
|---|---|
| Decision Scope | Constitutional rule or Tenant domain invoked. |
| Authorized Roles | Human roles responsible for the proposed gate. |
| Evidence Considered | Source-grounding entities used in the decision. |
| Conflicts Disclosed | Relevant disclosures from the Conflict Register. |
| Automated Assistance | AI modules used and their source grounding. |
| Decision and Rationale | Final disposition and human-authored justification. |
| Review/Appeal Path | Pre-defined contestability path for the action. |

This traceability path is intended to support later reconstruction. It does
not establish truth, record integrity, legitimacy, accountability, or
security by itself.

## 6. Layer 5: Bounded AI Assistance

AI is treated as a bounded analytical tool rather than a decision-maker. This
layer proposes calibrated reliance controls to reduce nominal oversight.

### Scope of assistance

Permitted tasks are advisory, such as document comparison, inconsistency
detection, and adversarial checking. The model does not grant AI authority to
approve its own use, resolve an appeal concerning its prior output, or make a
binding institutional disposition.

### Attribution and grounding

Every AI output should declare its source grounding, uncertainty level, and the
human reviewer responsible for its disposition. Source grounding means a
reconstructable link to the original Entity package in Layer 4; it does not
guarantee correctness.

AI-influenced decisions are proposed triggers for the risk and contestability
layer. The effectiveness of mandatory grounding and human disposition remains
an empirical question.

## 7. Layer 6: Risk Boundaries and Contestability

Institutional trust is treated here as a design objective supported by
contestability. This layer proposes a Policy Engine that identifies risk
conditions before a decision is treated as final.

### Risk boundary specification

A risk boundary identifies a triggering condition, such as a high-consequence
configuration, that requires escalation to a proposed independent role. That
role should have access to the original evidence package to support
reconstruction.

### Contestability workflow

1. Identify the applicable rule and risk boundary.
2. Challenge the specific evidence or rationale provided.
3. Request review by an independent role, including a fresh conflict
   disclosure.

This workflow is a proposed design pattern, not evidence of a standing
procedure, effective remedy, or guaranteed accountability.

### Institutional friction

To reduce denial-of-service risks against human reviewers, the model suggests
friction mechanisms such as Reputational Collateral or Sampling for Secondary
Review. These are unresolved design proposals that require legal, ethical, and
empirical review before any operational use.

## 8. Evaluation and Implementation Roadmap

The strategic utility of the architecture depends on staged evaluation of its
accountability-supporting properties in controlled environments before any
operational maturity is considered.

### Primary research questions

- **RQ1, Traceability:** Does the layered evidence model allow independent
  reviewers to reconstruct rationale more accurately than a comparison
  condition?
- **RQ3, Human-AI reliance:** How does mandatory source grounding affect a
  human reviewer's ability to detect errors in AI-generated summaries?

### Experimental protocol

Initial testing may use Wizard-of-Oz or high-fidelity mock-up interfaces. The
proposed studies would measure error detection rates and time to reconstruct
rationale. These are future evaluation criteria, not observed Axodus metrics or
results.

## 9. Limitations and Open Questions

This specification is conceptual and has not encountered implementation
constraints, institutional conflict, workload pressure, or field conditions.
It reports no deployment, users, adoption, empirical validation, production
use, security guarantee, financial operation, or legal conclusion.

Open questions include whether the six-layer decomposition is useful in
practice, whether the proposed records improve independent reconstruction,
whether human oversight remains substantive under workload pressure, how
reviewer independence should be established, and whether contestability paths
remain accessible without creating excessive procedural burden.

## 10. Research Agenda

Future work should develop interface artifacts, synthetic scenarios, and
controlled reviewer studies for reconstruction, cross-layer coherence,
challenge conditions, and bounded AI reliance. Later institutionally situated
studies would require separate governance, ethics, legal, privacy, and evidence
gates.

## 11. Conclusion

Axodus is proposed as a prototype-stage conceptual architecture for examining
how human-AI-assisted governance might become more traceable,
reconstructable, reviewable, and supportive of accountability and
contestability in federated modular digital institutions.

Its contribution is an inspectable six-layer arrangement linking
constitutional constraints, bounded local governance domains, functional
service modules, evidence and provenance, advisory AI assistance, and review
paths. The arrangement is a design proposal, not a product claim,
implementation report, validation result, or assertion of a uniquely correct
architecture.
