## Authoritative Source Policy

A future verification phase must use primary or authoritative bibliographic
sources whenever available. The preferred source hierarchy is:

1. publisher or journal official record;
2. DOI registration record;
3. Crossref or an equivalent authoritative metadata registry;
4. official author or institutional publication page;
5. trusted academic library catalog; and
6. indexed scholarly database as corroboration only.

The following must not serve as the sole metadata authority:

- retailer pages;
- blogs;
- user-edited bibliography websites;
- unsourced citation generators;
- AI-generated metadata;
- secondary summaries; or
- search-result snippets.

At least two independent authoritative records should be compared when
practical. Any material disagreement must be recorded rather than silently
normalized.

## Metadata Fields to Verify

For each candidate, the future verification record must address:

- full author names;
- normalized author order;
- exact title and subtitle;
- work type: article, book, chapter, or other;
- journal or publisher;
- publication year;
- volume;
- issue;
- page range;
- edition, if relevant;
- publication place only if required by the repository citation style;
- DOI;
- ISBN;
- stable publisher or journal identifier;
- canonical URL, if allowed by `../../governance/citation-policy.md`;
- language;
- publication status;
- reprint or edition ambiguity;
- exact BibTeX entry type;
- proposed citation key, without adding it to repository bibliography or
  manuscript files;
- metadata confidence level; and
- authoritative sources consulted.

Missing fields must not be inferred. The verification record must use `not
applicable`, `not found`, or `requires resolution` as appropriate.

## Proposition-Level Fit Boundary

Correct metadata is necessary but not sufficient for citation approval.
Before any source may be added, a later separately authorized phase must
identify:

- the exact manuscript proposition it would support;
- the exact section and paragraph;
- whether the source directly supports that proposition;
- whether the citation is descriptive, comparative, critical, or
  foundational;
- whether the citation would create a new claim;
- whether manuscript text must be revised;
- whether the addition expands the theoretical scope; and
- whether the source introduces political, operational, Academy, financial,
  legal, or governance-boundary risk.

No source may be added merely because it is influential, generally relevant,
or recommended by a reviewer.

## Deferral and Rejection Criteria

A candidate must be deferred or rejected if:

- authoritative metadata cannot be reconciled;
- the work or edition is ambiguous;
- the source supports only a new claim not currently in the manuscript;
- inclusion requires major manuscript restructuring;
- the citation is merely thematic rather than proposition-level;
- the candidate creates operational, financial, legal, Academy,
  political-rights, or governance drift;
- the source would be cited as prestige signaling rather than evidentiary
  support; or
- the candidate fails repository citation-policy requirements.
