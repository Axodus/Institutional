# v0.3 Author Review Brief

Review date: 2026-07-03  
Scope: internal author-review support for the v0.3 manuscript

## Executive summary

The v0.3 rewrite materially improves contribution unity over v0.2 by making
traceability and accountability in human-AI-assisted digital institutions the
dominant axis of the paper. The manuscript is now substantially clearer about
its prototype-stage status, its non-empirical posture, and the supporting
role of participation readiness, maturity separation, and risk boundaries.

The paper is suitable for serious author review. It is still not ready for
controlled external review because the bibliography remains thin in several
critical literatures, some supporting sections still occupy more space than
their current argumentative weight justifies, and the paper still reads as
broader than a strong externally circulated article.

## What changed from v0.2 to v0.3

- The title now foregrounds traceability and accountability rather than
  governance-oriented coordination more generally.
- The abstract now states a narrower research gap and identifies a more
  focused central contribution.
- The introduction now centers the paper on authority, evidence, bounded AI
  assistance, rationale, review paths, and contestability.
- Related-work framing was rebalanced toward traceability, accountability,
  calibrated reliance, and institutional reviewability.
- Participation readiness, maturity separation, and risk boundaries were
  repositioned as supporting components rather than headline contributions.
- The evaluation plan is now explicitly prioritized around `RQ1` first and
  `RQ3` second, with `RQ2`, `RQ4`, and `RQ5` treated as future work.
- The limitations section now more clearly states the lack of empirical
  validation, lack of deployment evidence, over-complexity risk, appeals
  risk, and calibrated-reliance risk.

## Contribution unity assessment

Assessment: improved and sufficient for author review, but not yet fully
disciplined for controlled external review.

Why:

- The paper now has a clear argumentative center: how a prototype-stage
  conceptual architecture could make institutional decisions more traceable
  and accountable under bounded human-AI assistance.
- The introduction, problem statement, evidence layer, governance/accountability
  section, evaluation priority, and conclusion now point in the same direction.
- The remaining drift comes less from the paper's thesis than from the amount
  of manuscript space still devoted to supporting strands.

## Remaining places where supporting strands still feel heavy

- `Modularity and Maturity as Supporting Controls`
  The section is better bounded than in v0.2, but it still reads like a
  partially independent contribution because it retains its own conceptual
  program and future specification agenda.
- `Participation Readiness as a Supporting Mechanism`
  The section is appropriately labeled as supporting, but its procedural flow
  and harm analysis remain comparatively detailed relative to its supporting
  role in this paper.
- `Risk Boundaries as Supporting Constraints`
  The section is narrower than before, but it still introduces a four-part
  framework that can read as a parallel contribution rather than a brief
  accountability support layer.
- `Research Agenda`
  The agenda remains useful, but multiple workstreams still remain visible at
  once, which reinforces the impression of a broad program rather than a
  single focused article.

## Over-broad or unsupported statements

No critical evidence-boundary breach was identified. The remaining issues are
editorial rather than compliance failures.

Points to watch:

- Some framing claims remain broad at the field level, for example the opening
  statement that digital institutions increasingly rely on automated
  assistance. This is plausible and not promotional, but stronger grounding
  would help if the paper later moves toward external review.
- The manuscript still depends on conceptual assertions about contestability,
  reviewability, and calibrated reliance that are directionally reasonable but
  not yet supported by the fuller literature base the paper now gestures
  toward.
- The paper appropriately marks several propositions as design proposals,
  hypotheses, or future work. Those distinctions should be preserved in any
  later revision.

## Related-work adequacy

Assessment for author review: sufficient.  
Assessment for controlled external review: insufficient.

Current strengths:

- The existing sources support the socio-technical, DAO governance,
  human-AI oversight, constitutional terminology, and verifiable-credentials
  baseline used in the manuscript.
- The related-work section is now more aligned with the paper's actual
  contribution than it was in v0.2.

Remaining gaps before any stronger external posture:

- contestability and appeals;
- calibrated reliance and override behavior under human-AI decision support;
- deliberative competence and informed participation;
- educational assessment validity, fairness, and accessibility;
- empirical DAO participation, delegation, and governance concentration;
- maturity-model reliability;
- Web3 regulatory and compliance constraints.

## Evaluation-plan assessment

Assessment: narrow enough for author review, still too expansive for stronger
external positioning.

Why:

- Prioritizing `RQ1` as the anchor study is the correct change.
- Keeping `RQ3` as a secondary study is defensible because it directly bears
  on accountable human-AI assistance.
- Moving `RQ2`, `RQ4`, and `RQ5` to future work materially improves focus.
- The paper still signals a broad downstream research program, so a later
  version intended for external review may need either a shorter evaluation
  section or a stricter split between immediate study design and later work.

## Limitations assessment

Assessment: strong enough for author review.

The limitations section now clearly addresses:

- lack of empirical validation;
- lack of deployment evidence;
- calibrated-reliance risks;
- contestability and appeals risks;
- participation exclusion risks;
- over-complexity and governability risk;
- regulatory and operational fragility.

The section is appropriately candid and no longer reads as a generic
placeholder.

## Terminology and interpretation risk

Current risk level: low for author review, still non-zero for any external
circulation.

Residual risks:

- `Proof of Knowledge` still appears once as an internal terminology note,
  which is acceptable internally but could still trigger confusion in an
  external academic setting.
- Token-linked and financial wording appears only as out-of-scope language,
  which is the correct boundary, but those terms remain visible in the paper.
- `Academy` does not appear in manuscript-facing text, which is the desired
  state.

## Bibliography gaps to close before controlled external review

- contestability and appeals;
- calibrated reliance in human-AI systems;
- deliberative competence and informed participation;
- educational assessment validity, fairness, and accessibility;
- DAO participation, delegation, and concentration;
- maturity-model reliability;
- Web3 regulatory and compliance constraints.

## Recommendation

Recommendation: **ready for author approval after minor edits**.

Meaning:

- Keep the paper internal for now.
- Use the present draft for serious author review.
- If the authors want a stronger next draft, focus on compression and
  bibliographic strengthening rather than adding new conceptual surface area.

This draft is not yet a candidate for controlled external review.
