## Overall assessment

In 150–300 words, summarize the paper's contribution, strongest architectural
feature, principal governance weakness, and most important revision:

Response:
The manuscript now communicates a clearer governance-centered contribution
than earlier lines: it proposes a prototype-stage conceptual architecture for
traceability, accountability, contestability, and reconstructability in
federated modular digital institutions under bounded human-AI assistance. Its
strongest architectural feature is the six-layer structure anchored by a
constitutional center and explicit evidence/provenance surfaces, which gives
reviewability a more concrete place in the model than a generic
governance-stack description would. The principal governance weakness remains
Section 4.3, where functional service modules are defined as bounded
institutional capabilities but then partially collapse into a taxonomy of
cross-cutting registries, review paths, and support components. That blurs
the difference between governed capabilities and the record/control elements
that cut across them. The most important revision is therefore not a broader
rewrite, but a bounded clarification of what a functional service module is
relative to evidence, review, and challenge structures, alongside a final
paper-facing terminology pass on residual `appeal` language.

## Contribution and architecture

1. State the paper's central contribution in one sentence. If this cannot be
   done, explain why.

Response:
Axodus is proposed as a prototype-stage conceptual architecture for
traceability, accountability, contestability, and reconstructability in
federated modular digital institutions under bounded human-AI-assisted
governance.

2. Does the manuscript remain centered on traceability, accountability, and
   contestability rather than drifting into ecosystem cataloging or governance
   branding?

Response:
Yes, substantially. The manuscript now stays centered on traceability,
accountability, contestability, and reviewability rather than on ecosystem
cataloging or institutional branding.

3. Are constitutional governance, bounded local governance domains,
   functional service modules, evidence and provenance, advisory AI
   assistance, and contestability differentiated clearly?

Response:
Mostly yes. Constitutional governance, bounded local governance domains,
evidence/provenance, advisory AI assistance, and contestability are
reasonably differentiated. Functional service modules remain the least stable
term because the current taxonomy still mixes bounded capabilities with
cross-cutting registries and review/control elements.

4. Which architectural relationship is missing, redundant, or ambiguous?

Response:
The relationship between functional service modules and the cross-layer
record/control elements remains ambiguous. Section 4.3 needs a cleaner
distinction between what counts as a governed capability and what counts as a
supporting architectural element that cuts across capabilities.

5. Does any passage still imply implementation inventory, active governance
   bodies, operational authority, or deployed institutional capability?

Response:
No explicit implementation inventory or active governance body claim is made.
However, residual references to `appeal`, `appeal conditions`, and an
`independent appeal role` can still sound closer to a standing regime than
the newer contestability framing elsewhere in the manuscript.

6. Is the distinction between conceptual governance design and implemented
   governance operation consistently preserved?

Response:
Mostly yes. The paper consistently frames the model as conceptual and
prototype-stage. The remaining pressure point is not an overt implementation
claim, but terminology drift that can momentarily make challenge and module
structures sound more institutionally settled than intended.

prototype-stage architecture should. The paper should keep challenge language
clearly conceptual and avoid mixing module abstractions with quasi-organizational
component taxonomies.
