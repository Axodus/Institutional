## Authorized Architecture-Layer Sections

### 4.1 Constitutional governance layer

- Current role in the manuscript:
  defines the higher-order roles, decision rights, prohibitions, amendment
  procedures, escalation paths, and appeal conditions that structure later
  local configuration.
- Planned modification type:
  controlled governance-abstraction refinement
- Allowed intervention:
  clarify the constitutional governance layer as a conceptual constraint
  surface; describe how it frames institutional rules, review procedures, and
  accountability boundaries; improve its relation to traceability,
  accountability, contestability, and bounded local governance domains at a
  conceptual level.
- Forbidden intervention:
  active enforcement language; legal authority claims; binding authority
  claims; active board or council claims; DAO claims; real institutional
  governance claims; implementation thresholds; operational procedures.
- Main claim risk:
  sounding like an active constitution or legal or institutional authority.
- Terminology risk:
  `constitutional` can drift from bounded governance abstraction into legal
  or deployed institutional implication.
- Evidence boundary:
  must remain a conceptual, prototype-stage governance layer and must not
  imply deployed authority, enforcement, or legal status.
- Markdown/LaTeX synchronization requirement:
  any conceptual refinement in `paper.md` must be mirrored in the `paper.tex`
  subsection with semantically equivalent constraint, authority, and review
  language.
- Author approval required before patch:
  no, if the patch stays conceptual and non-operational.

### 4.4 Evidence and provenance layer

- Current role in the manuscript:
  explains how proposals, supporting materials, authority statements,
  declared conflicts, AI-generated analyses, decisions, and later reviews are
  linked so that reviewers can reconstruct what happened and why.
- Planned modification type:
  controlled evidence/reconstructability refinement
- Allowed intervention:
  clarify that evidence and provenance support traceability and
  reconstructability; distinguish reconstructability from proof, integrity
  guarantees, truth guarantees, or complete accountability; clarify that
  records support later review rather than automatically validating
  decisions; align with the Phase 1.23 framing.
- Forbidden intervention:
  provenance-as-proof language; security guarantees; integrity guarantees;
  legal evidentiary claims; audit-certification claims; claims of implemented
  evidence infrastructure; production data or real usage examples.
- Main claim risk:
  overclaiming what provenance establishes.
- Terminology risk:
  `record`, `provenance`, and `reviewable` can be misread as claims of
  implemented evidence infrastructure or guaranteed auditability.
- Evidence boundary:
  must stay within reconstructability and review-support language only, with
  no claim of implemented evidentiary infrastructure or complete decision
  validation.
- Markdown/LaTeX synchronization requirement:
  provenance, reconstructability, and review implications must remain
  semantically equivalent across both manuscript formats.
- Author approval required before patch:
  no, if the patch remains boundary-preserving.

### 4.5 AI-assistance layer

- Current role in the manuscript:
  defines AI assistance as advisory support for bounded analytical and review
  tasks under retained human decision authority.
- Planned modification type:
  controlled AI-assistance boundary refinement
- Allowed intervention:
  clarify that AI assistance is bounded, advisory, review-supporting, and
  human-governed; clarify that AI may help summarize, classify, surface
  inconsistencies, support review, assist reconstructability, or provide
  planning-oriented review support; clarify that AI does not exercise
  autonomous authority, binding judgment, enforcement, or final institutional
  decision power.
- Forbidden intervention:
  autonomous authority; agent branding; named institutional agents;
  enforcement claims; security claims; guaranteed review quality; production
  workflow claims; claims that AI replaces human governance.
- Main claim risk:
  implying autonomous agentic governance.
- Terminology risk:
  the current phrase `operational planning support` can suggest workflow
  operation or implementation activity and should be treated as a wording risk
  for the later patch.
- Evidence boundary:
  must preserve bounded-assistance framing only and must not imply deployed
  assistants, governance automation, or operational decision execution.
- Markdown/LaTeX synchronization requirement:
  all AI-role, review-support, and human-authority statements must remain
  semantically equivalent across `paper.md` and `paper.tex`.
- Author approval required before patch:
  no, if no named agents or operational workflow claims are introduced.

### 4.6 Risk-boundary and contestability layer

- Current role in the manuscript:
  explains where review, correction, escalation, independence, and challenge
  remain possible after a proposed or recorded decision.
- Planned modification type:
  controlled contestability-boundary refinement
- Allowed intervention:
  clarify contestability as a conceptual review and challenge surface; explain
  that risk boundaries identify where escalation, review, refusal, or further
  evidence may be needed; describe contestability without implying a live
  appeals body, operating dispute process, active enforcement body, or
  binding review regime; align with the paper's
  traceability-accountability-contestability framing.
- Forbidden intervention:
  active appeals process; live moderation or dispute-resolution claims;
  binding decision procedure; enforcement workflow; legal or regulatory
  claims; implemented risk controls; metrics of effectiveness; claims that
  contestability prevents harm.
- Main claim risk:
  sounding like an operating governance procedure.
- Terminology risk:
  `appeal`, `review`, `independent role`, and `escalation path` can easily
  imply an active institutional process if not kept strictly conceptual.
- Evidence boundary:
  must remain a proposed governance-abstraction layer and must not imply a
  live appeals regime, active reviewer body, or implemented control system.
- Markdown/LaTeX synchronization requirement:
  contestability, escalation, reviewer-independence, and risk-boundary
  language must remain semantically equivalent across Markdown and LaTeX.
- Author approval required before patch:
  yes, because this section is high-risk and can easily imply operational
  governance.
